Experimental Analysis Report

Grokking: The Emergence of Generalization

Grokking is a phenomenon in deep learning where a model, after long periods of overfitting, suddenly transitions to perfect generalization.

The Experiment: Modular Addition

We trained a 1-layer Transformer on the task of (a + b) mod 97. Using only 30% of the possible data for training, we observe how the model moves from simple memorization to discovering the underlying mathematical rule.

Figure 1: Training loss (blue) drops to zero almost instantly, while test loss (orange) remains high until a sudden drop around epoch 7,000.

The Three Learning Phases

By tracking the L2 squared norm of the weights alongside the loss, we can categorize the training process into three distinct regimes:

1. Memorization

The model uses high weight norms to memorize training pairs. Test performance remains poor.

2. Circuit Formation

The model begins to discover a sparse, algorithmic circuit. Weights start to decrease due to weight decay.

3. Cleanup

The algorithmic circuit becomes dominant. Memorization is discarded, and the model groks the task.

Figure 2: The weight norm (green dashed line) peaks during memorization and then steadily declines as the model shifts toward a simpler, generalizing circuit.

Mechanistic Interpretability

How does the model actually solve modular addition? By applying a Fourier Transform to the embedding weights, we find that the model represents numbers as points on a circle.

The weights are not random; they are concentrated in specific frequencies. This confirms that the model has learned to implement a trigonometric identity for addition: cos(a+b) = cos(a)cos(b) - sin(a)sin(b).

Figure 3: 2D Fourier components of the weights. The sparse, symmetric peaks indicate that the model is using a small set of discrete frequencies to perform its calculation.

Emergence of Trigonometric Neurons and Circuits

To understand how this circular representation is processed, we inspect the activations of individual neurons in the MLP hidden layer. Instead of learning arbitrary look-up tables, individual neurons learn highly periodic, wave-like activations representing different spatial frequencies.

Figure 4: Activations of MLP Neurons 0 to 3 as a function of inputs a and b. The shearing and diagonal wave patterns show that neurons represent trigonometric components of the modular inputs.
Figure 5: Norm of the model's logits projected onto the 2D Fourier basis. The extreme sparsity indicates the model uses a tiny subset of key discrete frequencies (primarily frequency 24) to execute the arithmetic circuit.

Are these periodic neurons just a few isolated cases, or is the entire hidden layer behaving this way? By fitting a quadratic form of sines and cosines of the inputs to each neuron's activation pattern, we can compute the fraction of variance explained ($R^2$).

Figure 6: Histogram showing the fraction of variance explained by a quadratic fit on sinusoidal functions for all neurons. The massive spike near 1.0 confirms that almost the entire MLP layer represents structured trigonometric circuits rather than memorized tables.

Conclusion

This experiment demonstrates that generalization is not always a gradual process. In the presence of weight decay, models are incentivized to find the most "compressed" explanation for the data—which, in the case of modular arithmetic, is the mathematical algorithm itself.
